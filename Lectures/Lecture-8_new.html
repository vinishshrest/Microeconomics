The Elasticity of Demand

  • Firm maximizes profit by selecting a point where slope of the isoprofit curve (MRS) is equal to the slope of the demand curve (MRT)
  • Note that the isoprofit curve is the firms indifference curve.
  • The firms decision to increase or decrease price depends on how steep the demand curve is
    • in other words, how steep is the demand curve or how much would the consumer demand change due to the change in price.
  • The price elasicity of demand \(\epsilon = -\frac{\% \; change \;in \;quantity \;demand}{\% \;change \;in \;price}\)
    • \(\epsilon\) is the Greek symbol denoting elasticity
    • the negative sign in the formula is so that we get a positive \(\epsilon\). Note that \(\frac{\% \; change \;in \;quantity \;demand}{\% \;change \;in \;price}\) is negative
  • \(\varepsilon = -\frac{-5}{10}=0.5\)

The Elasticity of Demand and Slope

  • The elasticity of the demand is related to the slope of the demand curve. If the demand curve is flat then a small change in price will cause a larger proportion change in quantity demanded.

Figure 1. Shows demand curve

  • Figure 1 shows a demand curve with slope \(-80\)

  • \[\begin{align*} \text{slope of the demand curve} &= -\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta Q} \\ &= -80 \end{align*}\]

  • Since, \(\Delta P=-\$80\) when \(\Delta Q=1\), \[\begin{align*} \%\text{ change in }Q &= 100(\frac{\Delta Q}{Q}) = 100(\frac{1}{20}) = 5\% \\ \%\text{ change in }P &= 100(\frac{\Delta P}{P}) = 100(\frac{-80}{6400}) = -1.25\% \end{align*}\]

  • So, \[\begin{align*} \varepsilon &= -\frac{5}{-1.25} \\ &= 4 \end{align*}\]

  • Similarly, one can calcuate \(\epsilon\) at several points in Figure 1.

    • at \(Q=20\) and \(P=\$6400\), \(\epsilon = \frac{5}{-1.25}=4\).

  • Demand is elastic if the elasticity is greater than 1, inelastic if it less than 1 and unit elastic if its equal to 1

Elasticity of Demand and firm’s decision

  • Remember that the car manufacturer’s profit is maximized when \(Q=32\).
  • The firm would not choose a point \(D\) as this falls at the inelastic portion of the demand curve and the firm can always increase profit by increasing price (as the marginal revenue is positive at the elastic potion)
    • In other words, the firm would never produce at the inelastic portion of the demand curve.
    • A firm would choose a point where \(\epsilon>1\).

Market Faliure

  • The deadweight loss as shown in Figure 3 shows the extent of the market failure.
  • Market failure occurs when markets allocate resources in an inefficient way.
    • For example, in Figure 3, price could have been set at point F, where the profit margin is zero. But setting the price at a high margin deters people who otherwise would’ve bought the product for a cheaper price.
  • Note that the size of the deadweight loss depends on the elasticity of demand.
    • If the demand is inelastic the deadweight loss is larger compared to an elastic demand. For example the deadweight loss in Figure 3 is larger that the deadweight loss in Figure 2.
    • But what determines the elasticity of demand?

Competition or substitutes a Determinant of Elasticity

  • The elasticity of demand depends on how much of competition there is in the market.
  • If there exist huge competition, it means that there are a lot of substitutes
    • If a consumer can choose between several car providers, a consumer can move from a company (say Ford) to Honda if Ford increases the price.

Price-setting, competition, and market power

  • A manufacturer of a very specialized car will have quite a bit of market power.
  • Think about Tesla in this case. Tesla is highly specialized and different from other cars in the market
    • has less competition and will have less elastic demand
  • Can make monopoly rents
  • A firm will be in a strong position if competition is less (low substitutes).
  • If there are no competitors in the market, then there will be a single seller in the market.
    • In this case, this single seller will act as a monopoly
    • The monopolist will face a demand curve that is relatively more elastic. Here, a firm will set a price that is greater than the marginal cost of production.
